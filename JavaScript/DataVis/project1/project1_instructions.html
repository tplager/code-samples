Process

  1. Acquire and Clean Data Set: Choose a data set that you can work with, one that you feel comfortable that you can analyze and make sense of the information. Explore the data set and determine any deficiencies and issues that you will need to address by data cleaning using a tool like OpenRefine. These include:
    • Corrupt data
    • Duplicate data
    • Missing data
    • Non-normalized data (checking data is in same format, e.g. "1,000,000" vs. "1 million", or "Brazil" vs. "Brazil ")
  2. Analyze the Data Set: Look at the data and see what data variables (e.g. name, age, popularity) are present and what kinds of data they are (nominal, ordinal, discrete, continuous). Figure out which data variables are going to play a role in your visualization and what kind of visualization is best going to serve the data variables. Take notes on what you're seeing in the data, and check it once you work on your visualization.
  3. Develop your Visualization: Work with the data and D3 to build your visualization. Consider if the data variables you want to visualize are being served by the marks and channels you are using for your visualization. Additionally, check that your channels are working as you thought they would: are you getting visual interference? Is the visual representation of the data showing what you intend it to, and are the data variables really perceptible by the user? Are you using the best visual channels for importance of each data variable you are trying to represent? Also consider what you need to show directly to the user, and what secondary information you might reveal through interactivity. Finally, determine the number of visualizations you need to tell your story: you may need more than one visual to explain multiple facets of your story.
  4. Iterate: Developing a strong visualization is a process. Would mapping a data variable to a different channel make the data more easily perceptible? Are the most important data points being shown? After visualizing, are you finding that your original understanding of the data has changed and, if so, would a different visualization approach be better?
  5. Tell the Story: Develop the story for what you found in the data. The story should explain:
    • The big picture of the story
    • The details: describe the thought process that got you to the story. Use the visuals you have created to reinforce your message and show evidence for your conclusions. Use the visuals to have an impact on the reader/consumer.
    • Summarize: conclude your story.
  6. Finalizing Design: check out the style/design for your document and see what you can do to clean it up. Look at formatting, fonts, colors, style, etc. Like the visualizations, look to make the document as clear as possible for the reader to make the biggest impact possible.
  7. Do the Write Up: Finish your project by doing a write-up on the project devleopment process. What steps did you take? What did you find? Did you have any challenges?
